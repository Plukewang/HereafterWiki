# What Is Tempo?

Tempo is the primary driver of turn order in the Hereafter’s combat mechanics. Each player has a place in the turn stack that allows them to perform one or many actions of various types, and this place is determined by the tempo that results from the stack’s resolved effects.

  

While an effect is resolving for other players (e.g. taking damage, being occupied by a previously ongoing action, etc), players that do not have any resolving effects have a **tempo advantage**, or simply **tempo**. Players with tempo have the number and type of actions they can perform indicated by action tokens:

Black tokens represent allied actions, while red tokens represent enemy actions. Actions tokens displayed on the same line of the stack indicate simultaneity. For example, right indicator shows two allied actions and one enemy action occurring simultaneously.

  

![[example.png]]

Each simultaneous phase of actions in a turn resolves itself in stages:

1. Main Phase — All action tokens fully or partially expended during the phase resolve first.

1. Check Phase — All effects apply, update, and are checked (damage, modifiers, etc)

1. End Phase — Tempo shifts. Leftover action tokens are discarded save for exceptions.

As spells can affect the resolution of actions, they can be cast at any point during each of these phases once per turn. Invocations can be cast at any point during simultaneous phases of action, though with their usual ramping costs.

## Ways To Gain Tempo

As tempo determines the action economy of players, the fight for tempo is the first layer of complexity to The Hereafter’s combat mechanics. While countless plays and counterplays exist in the realm of players’ creativity, here are a few reliable methods:

- Forcing an opponent to mitigate an effect like damage can grant either a partial action icon or a full turn icon depending on the nature of the effect.

- Dodging or otherwise avoiding an action from the opponent grants typically grants a full action icon.

- Achieving significant damage on a player tends to grant a partial action token.

- Different spell types, such as <pass>, <feint>, and more can conditionally grant action tokens.

When up tempo on an opponent, actions can have three outcomes:

**The action presses the advantage**, and the player retains tempo, effectively applying further pressure to the opponent. This typically occurs in exchanges of blows where neither is the outright winner.

**The action resets the advantage**, and the player is at even tempo with the opponent. This typically occurs when the advantaged player achieves their goal but is unable to press further, such as landing a solid hit on an opponent who retreats. It is recommended for the formerly advantaged player to anticipate a counterplay from the opponent.

**The action loses the advantage**, and the opponent gains tempo. This happens usually when the opponent has effectively mitigated all of the advantaged player’s plays and can make their own play.
