### **Vul-Mot**

It began long before our ontological architectures, our great terraliths, and our dulcet, unending lives. It began before calamity befell the cradle of our civilization, where the trajectories of our fates were set irreversibly into motion.

Even before we had grasped the physical intricacies of our indeterministic universe, before we had comprehended as a species the schemes and mechanisms by which birds fly and we do not, before the first of the prophets spoke God into existence, we have first and foremost been the keen students of the Other; the things that lie beyond us.

 We observe the Other. We test the Other. We exploit the Other. We take from the Other. We pray to the Other. Over twenty millennia of human civilization, we have exhausted every single category of action we can commit upon the Other, again and again and again. And yet, as the scope of our understanding grows greater and the bounds of our knowledge ever expand, the Other remains, and we too remain with it, observing and testing and exploiting and taking and praying, again and again and again. Each time we uncover more of the terra incognita of our world, the Other grows in response to our exploration. Our acquisitions feed the Other as actions water the garden of fate, and so it is that in our time, the Other had grown larger than ever.

This is not surprising. We are not creatures who survive by looking down to the ground beneath our feet for survival, nor are our sights so single-mindedly focused toward the future that we have no room for rumination. When one's faculties are well-maintained and one's needs well-met, it is within our deepest instinct to look beyond, to the Other. Now, as every individual's faculties remain well-maintained and their needs well-met, looking to the Other has gone from a passing interest to a feverous worship. Thus did we, in our purported wisdom and plenty, surrender ourselves to the whims of the Other--to the whims of those who defined it.

These patterns of behavior will continue--must continue, so long as we remain human. That we continue to feed our relationship with the Other is human nature. We may only learn to curb the worse devils of that eternal liaison. Yet, how does one learn when there is no context? How can one gleam life from a corpse? It is for this reason that I leave this information to you, encoded to survive the distortion of history by the powers that be. Beyond all other details, know this: In our worship of the Other, we looked past the threshold of infinity to find a fragment of divinity, and so it was that we constructed our sophistic myth. Thus did we ruin ourselves.

We believed ourselves to have become the architects of our fates, and so enslaved ourselves to the greater design. We thought we could dam the flow of causality, that we could escape the fates that we had created. We were wrong, but there is no need to mourn us. We have received all that we have deserved and more; the ones to be mourned are you, the ones who will inherit the hysteresis of our fates and be punished for the crimes that you never committed. For what little it is worth, I am sorry, though I doubt there are many who would express such guilt now.

It began in the penultimate century before the Collapse, when an unmanned expeditionary vessel to the deepest hadal zone discovered an underwater cave and took back with it a picture of a painting--

### **Tot-Ah-Bit**

--painting perfectly preserved in the depths of a brine pool; no water damage, perfectly preserved strokes, and a vivid image of a black spear piercing a noise of color. No scholar could explain it, nor dared they, for the awakening of a new faith conceived in the wake of the mass confusion that followed the twentieth cenutry.

In the chaos of the Vietnam Wars, a new prophetic faith had arose; Amidst a wave of false memories and missing thoughts spreading like an invisible plague through the entirety of the human populace, one awoke from a feverish state with an awakened eye. Historical apocrypha suggests that the faith surrounding the so-called Inflorescence began with the doctors that woke Their Infloresence from their coma, only to be greeted by a completely different person in mind.

"This is all wrong. None of this should have happened." Said the prophet, in condemnation of the nature of the nonsensical chaos that surrounded them.

Unfortunately for us, the last of the line of prophets was also the one whose predictions would come true: Fission bombs, optic-cable networks, the connected world, Their Inflorescence spoke into the world technologies that the world had not yet conceived as if they should have been there all along. It was then, in the tone of the prophet's words and the chaotic leap forward that followed, that we as a species had found ourselves in a sinister place in the world.

In the madness of the years before the Fall, alongside humanity's ascent into the fever pitch of our collective delirium, we discovered that the ethereal holes in the sky we had once called stars were not of one category, but two: At the far-flung edges of the known universe, a darkness creeps and swells against the backdrop of celestial dust, heaving and swallowing it like plankton drawn into the mouth of a great cetacean; The Long Dark. The very universe was slowly inhaling itself, pulling apart the cores of its stars and reclaiming it into the vast oblivion 14 billion light-years away, leaving only bright glowing ghosts in its wake.

The ancient idea of stars as holes into the great Aether resurfaced, and the prophet did admonish their flock with great urgency. "You are staring into the edge of everything and anything, and you make it into a fetish. Stop it." But they did not stop. They could not stop, for with each passing moment, the pretense of reason and rationality was chipping away.

From that moment on, we could no longer avert our collective gaze from the end at the threshold of infinity. IT became our collective fixation, our utopia made manifest.

The question became simple: **What lies beyond in the hereafter?**

### **Was-Yesh Um-Guf**

The answer became our species' obsession--our ruin.

It brought us ruin when we gazed into the stars and found obliteration. It ruined us once more when the power to shape the universe fell into our idle hands. Now, ruin comes for us, broken upon the wheel of our worst's misgivings. Ruin threefold: victim, bystander, perpetrator.

It began in the weltering chaos of the post-Fall world, when humanity's inchoate shape yawned in strain as it stretched forth in all directions of the world, that it might rediscover the form it had lost. Earth's boiling seas returned to their blue fathoms, and its broken surface was mended by a newfound power--a power rooted in the mind's vacuum and nature's reprising abhorrence. I speak of the Aether.

In the chafing discomfort of a civilization born anew, on the same year as the last generation's annihilation did the first generation of scholars gather to discover that one of their own had in nocturnal fervor assembled a great tower of folded paper and glass, reaching beyond the ceiling of their lecture hall and into spaces unknown. Not one recoiled from the unraveling reality before them, and so they chose instead to construct. By the end of the week, the tower of paper and glass stretched and expanded outwards and upwards into its impossible space, countless fathoms up toward an incomprehensible sky. Of course, as is human nature, the scholars did ascend the tower, and in their ascent did they find ruin.

Baleful shadow-forms, gnashing and weeping of mouthless, voiceless things, and malformed shapes undue for this world awaited those who ascended the steps of the tower of paper and glass. In those early expeditions, three would enter and two would return, and so scholars did what scholars do: hypothesize, experiment, and categorize. Nonsensical rites became operating procedure and occult tomes became field guides. The mystical was mechanized, and in so doing darkly reflected the unfinished shapes of its explorers. Objects were recovered from the expeditions--never so unfinished and malformed as they appeared in the tower's heights: mechanisms that could call stars to rain from the sky, endless rolls of parchment on which indecipherable words transcribe, and a door to a dark, lightless plane that stretches for seeming eternity. Hypothesize. Experiment. Categorize. Each expedition's discovery was analyzed, understood, and put away, as one is wont to do as a scholar.

And, in that space of unfinished forms and gnashing teeth did we find the power to carve away the noise of existence into terrible malformed shapes, refashioned by nature in its abhorrence of vacuums. We called this stranger "Aether", and accepted it into our home as a permanent guest, smug and self-assured in our assumption of the intruder we had let in.

We constructed new towers with those same arcane rites and built upon the great passages lined with eyes to see deep into the recesses of the weeping void. We turned our eyes as we let its emptiness drink deep of our listless perception, gorging itself fat with the existence which it is without. We fed it feasts of our footprints trodden upon its blank spaces and showered it with the gifts of our speculative echolalia. In that gap between our incipient forms and this amalgam obliteration we had found power and luxury--matter creation, space travel, interconnectedness; we would reap from the gnawing void all that we had sown and more, as a farmer enjoys the fruits of his labor, or so we believed. In truth, the relationship between us and that lack-of-things (the Other) had always been purely transactional. Quid pro quo.

Thus, emboldened for decades by this endless wellspring of discovery did we turn our gaze toward that darkness in the far reaches of the universe once more, hoping to find for ourselves the image of what lies beyond life. Surely, if we can grasp the impossible, then we may yet reach the hereafter in this world, and not the next!

But we would not find that long dark, for the black of the sky would be obscured by--

### **Bit-Tot-Til**

--by that unspeakable everlasting. We should not have looked, and in looking we had our eyes pried open to a lidless horror. So, we blinded ourselves, forbade our amalgam eyes to cast their gaze upon that spot in the sky. Yet, in our insatiable greed and unbearable naivete, we believed ourselves prudent, that we may continue to gaze into that great unknown so long as we learned to avert our gaze from the visions that threatened to gaze back at us through the camera obscura of pinprick stars. We continued to look, to search, to allow ourselves to be reflected darkly, and reflected we were.

I do not know what my predecessors allowed onto our homes and our archives. I cannot know, even now.

The painting was lost to us in the depths of the ocean, and the skies were marred forever by our wrongful sight, but failure emboldens where success breeds complacency. We refined our eye-lined instruments and weaved them with quilted nerve-bundles, warp and weft interlocked by wordless curses and baleful chants in silent tongues. No longer satisfied with it as a mere resident, we endeavored to integrate the amalgam into our very lives, as deeply stitched into the patchwork fabric of our society as skin upon flesh. These things, vast swathes voiceless to cry suffering and mindless to conceive hate, or so we thought: an interface between us and the yawning void. In their thoughtless cognition they wielded a great and bloody knife, deftly carving rents and lesions into the face of reality as we saw fit, so that we may be closer to the Other, close enough to manipulate it with our clumsy fingers. We commanded the interfaces to raise great barriers to divide and categorize, and in doing so carved our mark upon the face of reality like the words upon the white stone of the world's cenotaph. The deal had been shook. There was to be no return.

That is its true name. The Cenotaph. Our cenotaph. Surprised? So was I. Don't bother asking around. No one uses that name anymore

So we went, hurtling around our precious **circuit 'round the Sun,** doing what we have always done. Wake, eat, rest, dream, work, embrace, defeat, sleep, depart, repeat. Our barriers grew more complex, as did the insignificance of our everyday troubles. Uplifted by the spiral of our ambition, the complacency that strangled our lives with leisure grew weltering in its limits like the pressure of an over-fermented cask. We unbound our lifespans; we extirpated our scarcities; we rendered every one of our ancestor's wildest dreams a poor simulacrum of our waking mundanity, and still we knew we were incomplete. The veil of survival instinct had been lifted, or so we believed, and yet we remained in this world and not that distant place, that hereafter beyond the Long Dark that still swallowed stars into its open maw. My forebears looked down upon their calloused fingers, swollen with bruises and laden with scars, and saw that all they had built had amounted to nothing in the scheme of infinity, not one atom of carbon off the infinite mountain of diamond. There was nothing left to do but behold the Other, so ubiquitous and enormous in its presence that it finally revealed its omnipresence to us like a god in the wait.

Two millennia of forward progress yielded nothing more than more refined interfaces and the colonization of our star's planets. The Terraliths, crowning jewels of my generation's spacefaring ambitions, masterpieces of the first great schools of ontokinetics, a thorn inscribed with a billion curses pierced into the heart of our star's gleaming children, that perverted, awful thing: we were so very proud of what we had made for ourselves. How the galleries cheered and celebrated as those stone vessels rammed a stake into the screaming heart of our rust-red earth and made it a vernal utopia. No more bombardment lakes. No more atmosphere converter plants. In the formless void we had gouged into the ground beneath our feet and the air that we breathe, our thoughts, filtered through the Terraliths, found purchase and dug in. "A home upon a hill and a boundless halcyon for each and every Man.” Paradise. How we comforted ourselves.

Some lies we told:

That the Terraliths could be modified in the case of contingency.

That there would be a continued effort to improve and refine their functions.

That their effects could be theoretically reversed.

That we would maintain the archives that held their core documentation.

That future generations could update or even decommission the Terraliths so long as they could establish their desired baseline in the proper language.

Lies told through the bared teeth of ostensible truth. Comforts based on technicalities. We genuinely believed that other world might emerge from the actions of our collective unconscious. The great schools—Ionia, Phoenicia, Sidh, Nihilo—posited that the Long Dark was proof that the semantic void of thought that awaited at the top of the Cenotaph's glass and paper tower was the selfsame void of far-flung space. At the boundaries of the cosmos, thoughts met rarefied matter as reality approaches the abstraction of raw, formless cognition. Their theory was thus: if the world to come awaited at the edge of physical existence, so remote as to be infinitely distant, then the gap could be bridged instead semantically, through the interface technologies our civilization had come to rely so heavily upon. We had substituted novelty for the ambiguous, and so came to rely upon the constant reuse and derivation of our own cultural ideals. Thus we deceived ourselves into constructing the Terraliths, under the thinly veiled pretext of terraforming. We knew the real reason, but acquiesced as a price for our pointless prosperity. With their power, we came to live on rocky moons turned miniature Earths and in the battering winds of the gas giants. Soon, the cradle of our civilization, our Earth, had become an eyesore in comparison to the idyllic worlds the Terraliths had created. Even the moon, once cratered and barren, outshone its host planet in its nocturnal lights.

When I first stepped from the manifold chambers of the first Solar Gun, having careened through the semantic void from Luna to Mars, I saw the Terralith for the first time, and I could help but—
