## Overview

Technique refers to the broad categorization of any skilled manipulation of any number of variables. The aforementioned definition is left deliberately broad, as _technique_ technically encompasses all forms of technique in the colloquial term. However, _technique_ in relation to the soul arts most commonly refer to the skilled manipulation of the interface between the self-as-will and all of existence, with a goal of reducing the number of abstractions between the self-as-will and the target of the _technique_.

> "Technique is merely the practice of minimizing the number of steps taken to reach a goal."

---


_Technique_ is divided at its broadest categories into _Apophatic_ (Negative) _techniques_, which approach the target goal by limiting the steps that can be taken, and _Cataphatic_ (Positive) _techniques_, which create a direct path to the target goal instead. Due to the normally positivist nature of most forms of consciousness, cataphatic techniques dominate the majority of technique practice, leaving apophatic techniques to the realm of outlier forms of consciousness and highly proficient technique practitioners. The typical uses of the two categories of technique reflect their respective popularity; cataphatic techniques range from the generation of mass and energy to the creation of novel logics and physical mechanisms, while apophatic techniques specialize in the exclusionary and insular—barriers, gateways, and complements. Oh, and black holes. Those too.

Both broad categories of technique rely on the use of communicable signs (semiotic processes) to reach their goals. Because practitioners attempt to reduce the number of these communicable signs needed to reach their target goal, technique refinement becomes an art of subtraction. However, it is important to distinguish that the number of signs in technique does not directly correlate with the number of concrete actions that take place for cataphatic techniques, which typically construct abstract steps toward the technique's target goal. As such, apophatic techniques require intimate knowledge of the mechanisms that govern concrete reality to operate at an equal efficacy to cataphatic techniques, with the inherent advantage of not requiring extra steps.

## Principles

### Relations between signs are intransitive.

> "Reality is a nested loop of complex rock-paper-scissor games played all at the same time."

Relationships assigned between two signs are not inherently associative, implying that two related relations do not automatically implicate the expected third relation. Signs vary from relations such as numerical comparison (which are transitive) and cyclical relations like rock, paper, scissors (which are antitransitive). Transitivity is a property that only emerges in apophasis down to a smaller category of signs.

**Thus, there exists a relation → such that if A → B and B → C, it is not always true that A → C.**

While considered counterintuitive by beginner practitioners, the lack of innate transitivity also affords a larger breadth of options for formulating their techniques. Apophatic techniques can be used to generate stronger properties like transitivity and antitransitivity, which a cataphatic technique can employ. Both aforementioned examples (ordered numbers and RPS) are ontologies constructed from the great intransitive set of communicable signs.

### Operations on signs are noncommutative.

> "Even retracing one's steps takes one back to a difference place than before."

The order of operations when performing any combinatory operation on two or more individual signs such within a technique matters. Any process by which one combines two signs to form a definite product of the signs will yield different results when done in a non-identical order, once again due to the small but nontrivial difference in steps between the orders of operation.

**Thus, there exists an operation x such that A x B ≠ B x A.**

The implications of this principle is not immediately apparent on the level of individual techniques, but prevent two practitioners of the exact same techniques from producing entirely identical results from their techniques, even if the individual signs are communicated identically. As such, a practitioner's personal path of learning plays a pivotal role in the final shape of their own technique. It is for this reason that highly proficient practitioners often have a wide range of personal experiences and revelations.

### The set of techniques is closed under abstraction

> "We cannot undescribe the indescribable, no matter how many times we try."

All communicable signs can be reached via other signs. The direct consequence of this principle is that omnipotence is theoretically* achievable via technique. Discussions of the principles of all technique rarely drawn the attention of beginner practitioners until the third principle is mentioned, upon which the eyes-glazed-over students will suddenly begin to pay attention again. Unfortunately for these would-be-gods, closure does not imply ease of access. While all goals are technically reachable, the two former principles ensures that each further step needed to access every possible goal becomes more convoluted than the next, leading to a loss of control in technique that sets an asymptotic limit on the capacity of any one practitioner.

**Thus, there is always an operation x that takes us from sign A to sign B.**

The convolution of steps is the primary cause of lifetime stagnation in seasoned practitioners, as they begin to exhaust their pool of available signs and technique sequences. To break through these plateaus of knowledge, one must be inoculated with novel communications that grant ingress to untraversed signs and goals. Stagnation and plateaus play a major role in the development of a practitioner, with the stagnation stage serving as a time to refine one's technique to its most complete in the form of a pinnacle technique, and the breakthroughs providing a one-time surge of novel power in the form of a technique transcendence.
  
\*It has already been achieved, but is no longer achievable.

## Uses

Technique usage is ubiquitous anywhere with sufficient interface between concrete and abstract reality. Cataphatic techniques serve as the basis of almost every form of sorcery, thaumaturgy, and other forms of paracausal manipulation, while apophatic techniques form the foundation of countless structures, such as selective barriers, curses, and other strongly-enforced contractual relationships. In most situations, technique practitioners operate within a framework formulated primarily from apophatic techniques, which supply the necessary rules and shared signs used in the cataphatic techniques that constitute the framework's directly observable manipulations. These frameworks seldom present outliers consistently enough that most practitioners do not see past the specifications of the frameworks themselves to the heart of technique, and as a result the pinnacle of the greatest practitioners exists simply as an approximation to the limits of the technique framework itself.

The transcendence of a practitioner's technique beyond their pre-existing frameworks typically occurs as a result of a sudden perception of the apophatic techniques that underlie the foundations of the technique framework—a realization often accompanied by the development of amateur apophatic techniques like simple barriers. As technique is an art of subtraction in its essence, these simpler apophatic techniques usually trump the added complexity of cataphatic techniques formulated by an external framework, leading to a perception that the discovery of technique beyond a practitioner's own pre-existing frameworks is a form of upgrade.

Despite appearances, the true nature of rediscovering technique is that it allows for one to induce effects with greater finesse and control, allowing a practitioner to adopt and even craft frameworks to suit the weaknesses and strengths of their pursuit. Thus, there is no such thing as an objectively superior technique, only one that is more refined for its purpose.
